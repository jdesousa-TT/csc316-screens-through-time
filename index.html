Our Data Story

Streaming platforms showcase diverse genres, but not truly diverse production. By comparing genre, language, and location, this project shows that global catalogs are dominated by a few regions and languages. The visualizations presented uncover this gap, pointing to an untapped opportunity for growth in audience reach and content investment in underrepresented regions.

What the Data Told Us

Streaming platforms create the appearance of global diversity, but our visualizations show a different reality. Across genres, the same languages and production regions continue to dominate what audiences see. At the same time, large, underrepresented markets show clear audience demand and revenue potential. This gap suggests that the future of streaming isn’t just about more content, but about expanding whose stories are produced, distributed, and scaled globally.

Language Representation Over Time

How has the diversity of languages on Netflix changed across decades?

For decades, English was the default language of all media, and the stories that reached the world were usually almost American or British. But scroll through Netflix today, and it is completely different. This visualization tracks the language origins of streaming content across four decades: the 1980s through the 2010s. Each small square represents a portion of titles from that decade. The pattern that emerges is that English is still the dominant language, but it's so much less so. South Asian and East Asian content has grown substantially since the 1980s, reflecting the rise of Bollywood, Korean Dramas, and anime. Spanish-language content has also grown alongside Arabic and Turkish titles.

Genre & Language Connections

How do film genres relate to their original language? Flow width shows the number of films.

Every genre has a language it calls home. Drama and Comedy produce the most titles overall, but the story becomes more interesting when you trace where that content actually comes from. English dominates across nearly every genre — not because other languages lack the stories, but because the industry has historically concentrated production in a small number of markets.

Look closer and patterns emerge. French cinema leans toward Drama and Romance. Japanese titles cluster in Animation and Horror. Hindi-language films are heavily represented in Action and Romance, reflecting the scale of Bollywood. These are not random distributions — they reflect decades of cultural tradition, studio infrastructure, and audience expectation.

Budget vs Revenue

Did the budget pay off? Arrows show each film's return on investment

Not every big budget pays off. Studios routinely pour hundreds of millions into sequels and blockbusters, assuming that bigger spectacle means bigger returns. But the data tells a more complicated story. Some of the most expensive films ever made barely broke even, while modestly budgeted productions quietly returned many times their cost. Genre matters more than spending. Drama and Fantasy consistently deliver the highest return on investment, often outperforming the Action films that dominate the catalog by sheer volume. English-language productions account for most of the total revenue, but that advantage narrows when measured by ROI — smaller films in unexpected languages occasionally punch far above their weight.

Platform Dominance in Global Streaming Catalogs

Comparing where Netflix, Amazon Prime, and Disney+ source titles worldwide

Streaming platforms are often described as global, but the origins of the content within their catalogs are not evenly distributed around the world. This visualization reflects how different regions contribute to the libraries of major streaming services such as Netflix, Amazon Prime, and Disney+. By comparing where titles are produced, it highlights patterns in platform dominance and reveals which parts of the world supply the most content. On the globe, each country displays colored markers for the platforms, where larger markers indicate a greater number of titles linked to that country. These patterns suggest that while streaming platforms distribute content globally, the production of that content is still concentrated in a smaller set of influential regions. Together, these trends connect to the broader theme of our project: although streaming has expanded access to international media, the industry continues to be shaped by regional production hubs.
