Our Data Story

The Gap Behind Global Catalogs

Streaming platforms showcase diverse genres, but not truly diverse production. By comparing genre, language, and location, this project shows that global catalogs are dominated by a few regions and languages — and points to an untapped opportunity for growth in underrepresented regions.

For decades, English was the default language of all media. But scroll through Netflix today and it looks completely different. This visualization tracks the language origins of streaming content across four decades — the 1980s through the 2010s. The pattern that emerges is that English is still dominant, but much less so. South Asian and East Asian content has grown substantially, reflecting the rise of Bollywood, Korean Dramas, and anime.

Every genre has a language it calls home. Drama and Comedy produce the most titles overall, but the story becomes more interesting when you trace where that content actually comes from. English dominates across nearly every genre — not because other languages lack the stories, but because the industry has historically concentrated production in a small number of markets.

Streaming platforms distribute content globally, but producing it remains a regional affair. This visualization maps the origins of titles across Netflix, Amazon Prime, and Disney+, revealing which countries dominate each platform's catalog and where production is concentrated. The patterns reinforce a central tension in global streaming: while access has expanded worldwide, the industry's creative output continues to flow from a narrow set of production hubs.

Conclusion

What the Data Told Us

Streaming platforms create the appearance of global diversity, but our visualizations show a different reality. Across genres, the same languages and production regions continue to dominate what audiences see. At the same time, large, underrepresented markets show clear audience demand and revenue potential. This gap suggests that the future of streaming isn't just about more content — but about expanding whose stories are produced, distributed, and scaled globally.
